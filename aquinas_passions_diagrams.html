2. The Eleven Passions

Passions are individuated by their formal objects. Concupiscible passions respond to good and evil simply; irascible passions respond to the arduous — good or evil that is difficult to attain or avoid.

4. Passivity ≠ Loss of Control

King identifies two fallacies about passivity that Aquinas's theory avoids.

Fallacy 1
"Passivity means we are at the mercy of our passions"
Passions are passive potencies — actualized by external principles
But: the sensitive appetite is a part of the soul, not the whole
The will (intellective appetite) is a co-cause of the soul's overall state
Analogy: a wound to the hand doesn't cause the rest of the body to be wounded
Fallacy 2
"Passivity means the passions are involuntary"
Actions from passions satisfy both conditions for voluntariness: internal principle + known end
The will must consent to a passion for it to serve as basis for action
Exception: when passion literally overwhelms reason (rare) — reduces humans to level of brute animals
Ordinarily: agent "goes along" with passion, retaining free judgment

5. Passion, Voluntariness, and Moral Responsibility

Actions done from passion range from fully voluntary to (rarely) non-voluntary. The type of passion matters for culpability.
